Birth Control (1966)

Birth Control is a German progressive and hard rock band formed in 1966. Their music depicts heavy sound, complex compositions, and satirical lyrics. Despite lineup changes over the decades, Birth Control continued to evolve, blending elements of psychedelic rock, jazz, and blues into their music. While they never reached mainstream international fame, their influence on European rock is undeniable, making them a great but often forgotten band. Their music paved the way for many of today's progressive and hard rock bands, and their albums still hold a special place in the hearts of dedicated fans. Birth Control was ahead of its time, and their legacy continues to inspire, but more commercially successful bands often overshadow them. That's why they are both great and forgotten—lost in the shuffle of time, but deserving of a more prominent spot in the history of rock music.

I also love their unique album covers, which is a heavily underrated topic. Their album art often played with surrealism, abstract concepts, and sometimes humor, adding another layer of intrigue to their music. Unfortunately, album covers often take a backseat to the music itself in discussions of band legacies, but Birth Control's covers are an essential part of their identity, adding a visual element to their already dynamic sound. They remain an underrated topic, but when you dive into their discography, the artwork becomes an integral part of the experience, creating a deeper connection to their music and their message.

Can (1968)

Can is one of the most influential yet underrated bands in rock history. As key figures in the Krautrock movement, they redefined what rock music could be by blending avant-garde experimentation, jazz-inspired improvisation, electronic textures, and hypnotic grooves. Their music pushed boundaries in ways that were far ahead of their time, laying the groundwork for numerous genres, including post-punk, ambient, electronic, and even hip-hop sampling.

Despite their profound influence, Can remains largely unknown to mainstream audiences, often overshadowed by more commercially successful rock bands. They lacked conventional hits, favoring long, hypnotic jams over radio-friendly singles. Their music, rooted in improvisation and inspired by avant-garde composer Karlheinz Stockhausen, was fluid, unpredictable, and hard to categorize. Their embrace of noise, electronic elements, and unconventional time signatures distanced them from commercial rock radio.

Emerging from West Germany in the late 1960s, Can was labeled as "Krautrock," a term that initially marginalized them. Unlike their blues-influenced contemporaries like Led Zeppelin and The Rolling Stones, Can operated outside traditional rock structures. They recorded in their own studio, Inner Space, and never chased mainstream success, staying true to their experimental vision.

Yet, they deserve far more recognition. Bassist Holger Czukay pioneered tape editing and sampling, influencing future electronic and hip-hop production. Drummer Jaki Liebezeit's hypnotic, precision-driven "motorik" beat became a blueprint for post-punk and electronic music. Can constantly reinvented themselves—Monster Movie (1969) was raw garage rock, Tago Mago (1971) delved into psychedelic madness, Future Days (1973) embraced ambient textures, and Soon Over Babaluma (1974) explored world music.

Their music still sounds futuristic today. Unlike many '70s bands that feel dated, Can's innovative production and unconventional song structures make their albums as fresh as ever, proving they weren't just ahead of their time—they were timeless.

Embryo (1969)

Despite their profound influence, Embryo remains one of the most overlooked and underappreciated bands of the progressive rock era. Unlike many of their German contemporaries, who leaned into hypnotic repetition and electronic textures, Embryo took a different path—one of constant evolution, cultural exploration, and boundary-pushing experimentation. They weren't just a band; they were a musical collective, fusing jazz, rock, and world music in ways that still feel ahead of their time.

Formed in 1969 by multi-instrumentalist Christian Burchard, Embryo never fit neatly into any category. While they emerged from the "Krautrock" movement, their restless spirit took them far beyond it. They toured extensively across Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, collaborating with local musicians and incorporating new sounds into their music. While Pink Floyd and Genesis perfected their studio polish, Embryo thrived on raw, organic improvisation, making every performance unique. Their music was alive, ever-changing, and untamed.

They deserve far more recognition, not just for their musicianship but for their fearless approach to music. Burchard's percussion work was mesmerizing, blending jazz fluidity with tribal rhythms. Their albums defied convention—Rocksession (1973) was an electrifying jam-based masterpiece, We Keep On (1973) pulsed with hypnotic grooves and intricate solos, and Embryo's Reise (1979) was a revolutionary project that documented their journey across continents, blending traditional folk music with progressive rock, that is why this website provides this album with timestamped table. Embryo to me is truly timeless explorers, proving that music has no borders.

Focus (1969)

While many '70s rock bands leaned into blues-based structures or conceptual storytelling, Focus took a different approach—blending classical music, jazz improvisation, and rock virtuosity into something both sophisticated and wildly energetic. Their music was dynamic, unpredictable, and at times downright eccentric, yet their technical brilliance was undeniable. While many '70s rock bands leaned into blues-based structures or conceptual storytelling, Focus took a different approach—blending classical music, jazz improvisation, and rock virtuosity into something both sophisticated and wildly energetic. Their music was dynamic, unpredictable, and at times downright eccentric, yet their technical brilliance was undeniable.

Formed in the Netherlands in 1969 by guitarist Jan Akkerman and keyboardist/flautist Thijs van Leer, Focus stood apart from their British and American counterparts. While bands like Genesis focused on intricate, sprawling compositions, Focus balanced sheer instrumental prowess with an unmistakable sense of fun. Their ability to fuse baroque-style melodies with heavy rock riffs, yodeling, and flute solos made them one of the most unique progressive bands of their time.

They deserve far more recognition, not just for their musicianship but for their willingness to break conventions. Akkerman's guitar work was breathtaking—both fluid and fiery—while van Leer's keyboard and flute contributions gave the band a distinctive, almost medieval character. Hocus Pocus (1971) remains their most famous song, a jaw-dropping blend of shredding guitars, yodeling vocals, and relentless energy that sounds as exhilarating today as it did upon release. Round Goes The Gossip (1972) showcased their playfulness and compositional skill, while Sylvia (1972) was a rare moment of elegant, melodic beauty highlighting Akkerman's lyrical guitar playing. They weren't just ahead of their time; they were in a world of their own—one where rock could be both technically brilliant and unashamedly fun.

Thunder Statue (1992)

Thunder Statue, a pioneering Georgian band, remains one of the most overlooked gems in the progressive rock scene. Despite their exceptional musicianship and bold sound, they've never received the recognition they truly deserve outside their home country. Blending elements of hard rock, progressive, and experimental music, Thunder Statue's compositions were intricate, emotive, and filled with intensity. Their music spoke to a deep connection to both the natural world and the struggles of human existence, weaving traditional Georgian influences with global rock trends to create something both unique and compelling.

Formed in the late 1980s, Thunder Statue was part of Georgia's underground music scene during a turbulent period in the country's history. As the Soviet Union crumbled and the political landscape shifted, the band captured the sense of uncertainty and transformation in their music. Their ability to blend folk-inspired melodies with progressive rock's ambitious structures made them stand out among their contemporaries. Yet, despite their powerful sound and originality, they never quite broke through, neither in international recognition nor even in the country.

Thunder Statue deserves more attention for their fearless approach to music. They combined traditional Georgian rhythms with complex guitar work, intricate drum patterns, and soaring vocals, creating a sound that was uniquely their own.
